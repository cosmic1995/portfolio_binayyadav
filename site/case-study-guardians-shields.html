Overview


Guardian's Shields is a healthcare product. At Omnicom Media Group, I led the complete redesign of the product website — applying a human-centered design approach that combined user research, iterative usability testing, and accessibility compliance.

The outcome: a 50% increase in site engagement, lower bounce rates, and recognition internally as a reference standard for healthcare client UX work.


Healthcare UX redesign: restructuring around the user's journey, not the product's feature list.

The Problem

The previous website was structured around the product rather than the person using it. It opened with features, technical specifications, and pricing — a logical sequence from a sales perspective, but entirely the wrong sequence for a healthcare audience.

Healthcare users arrive in a research mindset. They are trying to understand whether this product is relevant to their situation — evaluating the problem, not yet comparing solutions. The existing architecture assumed they had already decided they needed this category of product and were now in a buying decision.


The engagement data confirmed it: the site was losing people at the top of the journey, before the product had a chance to establish credibility.

Research

Healthcare decision-maker interviews Trust formation research User personas Accessibility audit (WCAG)

Sessions covered three areas: how users orient themselves on an unfamiliar healthcare product page; what questions need to be answered before trust forms; and where the existing navigation caused exits. An accessibility audit ran in parallel, identifying contrast failures, focus order issues, and unclear labelling.

The Redesign

The information architecture was rebuilt around the mental model the research surfaced:

Understand the problem → Trust the product → Make a decision → Take action.

Navigation: 8 → 4 top-level items Lead content rewritten around user context WCAG accessibility compliance Multiple usability testing rounds

Navigation restructured around the questions users were actually asking. Lead content rewritten to address the user's situation before describing the product. Accessibility brought to WCAG compliance across contrast, focus, labels, and keyboard navigation.


Before: product-first. After: user-context-first — meeting users where they are, not where the company needs them to be.

The Result


50% increase in site engagement.

Time on site increased. Bounce rate decreased. Conversion to the contact and information pages improved significantly. The redesign was recognised internally at Omnicom as a reference standard for healthcare client UX work.

Reflection

Healthcare UX makes the stakes of information architecture unusually visible. A user who leaves a retail site without finding what they need has a minor inconvenience. A user who leaves a healthcare site without understanding whether a product is right for their situation may make a worse decision as a result.


Designing for that context required holding two things simultaneously: the emotional state of the user — uncertainty, a need for reassurance before information — and the practical requirements of what they needed to know. Getting the sequence right mattered more than getting the content right. The right information in the wrong order still loses people.
