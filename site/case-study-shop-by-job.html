Shop by Job

Led the end-to-end design of a job-first purchase flow for Lowe's Pro contractors, delivering a 20% increase in transaction completion rates.

For

Lowe's India Pvt. Ltd

My role

Lead UX Designer · Full ownership · Concept to launch

Tags

Product Design · UX Research · Usability Testing · Enterprise

Year

2025

Product Design UX Research Usability Testing Enterprise

Overview

Lowe's Pro customers — professional contractors who run active job sites — are among the company's highest-value segment. They buy in volume, they buy repeatedly, and they know exactly what they need. The platform, however, was not built for them.

Shop by Job is a feature I identified, proposed, and designed from scratch. It restructures the Pro purchase journey around the job a contractor is doing — not the product department where the material happens to live — closing the gap between how contractors think and how the platform works.

The result was a 20% increase in transaction completion rates among Pro customers.

The Problem


Backwards — The platform was organised by product departments, while contractors think in jobs (e.g., "rough-in plumbing for a two-bath remodel").

Exhausting — Building a full materials list required multiple category hops, manual tracking, and constant context switching.

Unforgiving — Incomplete orders mean job delays, rework, and costly return trips — the stakes are higher than consumer shopping.

The Opportunity

This is not a UI problem. It is a mental model problem.

Backwards → Job-first
Reorganise the purchase entry point around what a contractor is doing, not what product category they are browsing. If the platform starts with the job, every subsequent decision becomes faster and more confident.

Exhausting → Streamlined
Reduce the steps between job context and checkout to the minimum necessary. The ideal path: choose the job type, adjust quantities, confirm order. Three decisions, not thirty.

Unforgiving → Reliable
Design the feature so a complete project materials list is the default output — not something the contractor has to construct manually from memory.

Design question: How might we let a Pro customer start with the job they're doing and arrive at a complete, ready-to-order materials list in as few steps as possible?

Who This Was Designed For

Not a consumer browsing options. A professional with a schedule, a crew, and a client waiting.

Pro contractors have a fixed job type, they know their material quantities from experience, and they expect a professional-grade tool that reflects how they actually work. Their tolerance for a platform that treats them like a first-time retail shopper is zero.

They plan purchases by job type, not by product category. They drop off when the platform doesn't match how they think.

Research & Discovery

Secondary research Contractor sessions Journey mapping Competitive analysis

I initiated research before a formal brief — combining secondary data, contractor sessions, journey mapping, and competitive review to validate the problem and frame it as a business opportunity.

The key insight: the pain wasn't just too many steps; it was that the flow started in the wrong place. Contractors hold a job context in their head while the platform asked them to think in product categories. The solution had to start with the job.

Design Process

What I started with — and scrapped

The first version of the flow started at the product level — an improved search experience with better filtering for Pro users. I scrapped it.

The research had made something clear: a better search is still a search. It still requires the contractor to know the product name, navigate to the right department, and build the cart piece by piece. Improving the existing model was solving the wrong problem. The feature needed a different entry point entirely.

The model that replaced it

The research finding pointed directly at the solution: contractors organise their work by job type, so the feature had to start there. I designed the flow around a simple decision tree:

What job are you doing? → Here are the materials you'll need. → Adjust and order.

The six job categories — Plumbing, Electrical, Painting, Flooring, Drywall, Deck/Outdoor — were validated against contractor research and existing Pro purchasing data. Categories that seemed logical from a product perspective but didn't match how contractors talk about their work were excluded. This required direct conversation with the product and merchandising teams to align on what "job-first" actually meant in practice.

Design system alignment

All components were built on Backyard Design System 4. This kept the feature visually consistent with the broader Lowe's Pro experience and reduced handoff friction with engineering — specifications were tied directly to existing DS4 tokens and components rather than bespoke styles.

The Solution

Job selector Pre-built materials list Direct-to-cart 3-step flow

Select the job → get a pre-built materials list → adjust quantities → add the full order to cart. Three decisions, not thirty, and no product-category detours.

Testing & Iteration

Job-first model validated; redundant review step removed.

Testing confirmed contractors immediately understood the job-first entry point. What didn't work: a redundant review screen between the materials list and cart that interrupted the natural flow without adding decision value.

Removing it collapsed the flow from five steps to three. Completion improved in the follow-up round and qualitative feedback shifted from hesitant to confident.

The Result


Measured against the previous individual-search flow for the same customer segment.

A 20% increase in transaction completion rates among Pro customers — Lowe's highest-value segment. For the business, it meant recovered revenue on journeys that previously dropped off. For contractors, a purchase flow that finally matched how they actually plan work.

Strategic Considerations


What it took beyond the design work.

I proposed this project

This was not a brief handed to me. I identified the problem, structured the research, presented the case for building it, and got alignment from the product and operations teams before any design work started. Making that case required framing the problem as a business problem — not a UX complaint — and showing the opportunity in terms of completion rate improvement before there was any data to prove it. That framing shaped how the project was received, resourced, and prioritised.

The taxonomy was a constraint, not a creative decision

The six job categories — Plumbing, Electrical, Painting, Flooring, Drywall, Deck/Outdoor — were validated against contractor research and existing Pro purchasing data to ensure the categories reflected actual job patterns. Categories that seemed logical from a product perspective but didn't match how contractors talk about their work were excluded. This required direct conversation with product and merchandising teams to align on what "job-first" actually meant in practice.

I scoped the MVP deliberately

The decision to start with six job types — rather than building the full range — was a deliberate trade-off between launch speed and coverage. A feature that launches well for six job types builds more trust with Pro customers than a feature that launches inconsistently across twenty. The data from the initial six will inform the expansion roadmap.

Reflection

What I'd do differently, and what this project taught me.

I would have done more direct contractor sessions earlier. I made a pragmatic call — secondary research plus one or two direct interviews was enough to validate the core insight and move into design. That call was right for the timeline. But the usability testing revealed a flow problem (the redundant review screen) that earlier qualitative sessions would likely have surfaced. The extra sessions would have cost a week. Removing the review screen at the testing stage cost more.

The self-initiation was the hardest part. Designing a feature is one skill. Making the case for it from scratch — to product managers, operations leads, and development teams who have other priorities — is a different skill. I had to learn how to translate a design observation into a business argument before I had any data to support it. That experience changed how I approach every project since: I now write the business case before I write the design brief.

The taxonomy insight surprised me. I expected the main research finding to be about steps — too many clicks, too long a path. But the deeper finding — that the problem was not the length of the path but where it started — was not what I was looking for. I found it because the contractor sessions kept returning to the same moment: the moment the platform asked them to think in product categories instead of job context. The most useful research findings are often not the ones you designed the research to find.
